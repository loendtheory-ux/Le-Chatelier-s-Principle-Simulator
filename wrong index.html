📜 History of the Periodic Table

Topic 1
🏛️
How the Periodic Table Was Built
Lavoisier → Mendeleev → Moseley
Elements like gold, silver, copper, and iron were known to ancient people because they exist as pure metals in nature — visible, shiny, and easy to work with. Other elements required chemical reactions to isolate, which ancient civilizations lacked the tools to do.
⚗️Lavoisier (1700s): Defined "element" as a substance that cannot be broken down further by chemical means. First list of ~33 elements.
🧩Mendeleev (1869): Arranged elements by ATOMIC MASS — noticed periodic (repeating) patterns in properties. Left gaps for undiscovered elements!
☢️Moseley (1913): Used X-ray spectra to prove atomic NUMBER is the true organizing principle. No more gaps!
🔑 Evidence Mendeleev was right: He predicted properties of undiscovered elements (gallium, scandium, germanium). When they were found, his predictions matched almost perfectly!
💡 Modern table: Arranged by increasing atomic number (= # of protons). Periodic Law = properties repeat as periodic functions of atomic number.

Know the three scientists in order: Lavoisier (defined element) → Mendeleev (arranged by mass, predicted gaps) → Moseley (arranged by atomic NUMBER). The Regents often asks why Mendeleev's table was revised — answer: atomic number is the true organizing principle, not mass.

🔬 Moseley's Law

Henry Moseley fired X-rays at metal targets and measured the frequency of X-rays emitted. He found a mathematical relationship between X-ray frequency and atomic number (Z). This proved that the periodic table should be arranged by proton number, not mass. It also explained why a few elements seemed "out of order" in Mendeleev's table (e.g., Ar before K).

🏅 Noble Prize Impact: Mendeleev nearly won the Nobel Prize but died before it could be awarded. His predictive table changed science forever.
⚒️ Gallium Prediction: Mendeleev called it "eka-aluminum" and predicted its melting point, density, and reactions — all confirmed when Lecoq de Boisbaudran discovered it in 1875.

Why did Mendeleev's original table have some elements "out of order" by mass but still grouped correctly by properties? What did Moseley's work reveal about this?

If you discovered a new element today, what information would you need to place it correctly on the periodic table? What would you predict about its properties before even testing them?

🗂️ Organization: Periods & Groups

Topic 2
📊
Periods and Groups
Rows and columns of the periodic table
↔️PERIOD = Horizontal row (runs left to right) — 7 periods total
↕️GROUP = Vertical column (runs up and down) — 18 groups total
Period number = number of occupied energy levels (shells) an atom has.
Group number (for main group elements, Groups 1–2 and 13–18) = number of valence electrons.
📅 Calendar Analogy: Think of periods like rows of a calendar (weeks). Elements in the same GROUP are like the same day of every week — they share similar properties because they all have the same number of valence electrons!
💡 Elements in the same GROUP share similar chemical properties. Elements in the same PERIOD share the same number of energy levels but different numbers of valence electrons.

The Regents will tell you an element is in Period 3, Group 2 — you must be able to state: 3 occupied energy levels, 2 valence electrons, metal (alkaline earth). Also know: period # = # of electron shells; group # (for main groups) = # of valence electrons.

🔬 Period ↔ Energy Level Connection

The period number directly tells you how many principal energy levels (shells) are occupied. A Period 2 element like Li has electrons in shells 1 and 2. A Period 4 element like K has electrons in shells 1, 2, 3, and 4. This is why atomic radius INCREASES going down a group — each row adds a whole new energy shell farther from the nucleus.

📱 Silicon & Semiconductors: Silicon (Group 14, Period 3) has 4 valence electrons — exactly why it can form 4 bonds and works perfectly as a semiconductor in computer chips.
🧂 Salt Formation: Sodium (Group 1) always forms Na⁺ and chlorine (Group 17) always forms Cl⁻ — predictable from their group positions alone!

🔬 Categories of Elements

Topic 3
⚗️
Metals, Nonmetals & Metalloids
The "staircase" divides the table
🔩METALS — Left of/below staircase (except H). Most of the table!
Metal properties: malleable (hammered into sheets), ductile (drawn into wire), have luster (shiny when polished), good conductors of heat & electricity (due to "sea of mobile valence electrons"), mostly solids at STP — except Hg (mercury) which is a liquid. Metals LOSE electrons → form positive ions (CATIONS).
Exception: Hydrogen is in Group 1 but is a NONMETAL!
💨NONMETALS — Right of/above staircase
Nonmetal properties: NOT malleable/ductile — brittle (shatter easily), dull luster, poor or non-conductors of electricity. Mostly gases at STP, except bromine (liquid) and iodine, carbon, sulfur (solids). Nonmetals GAIN electrons → form negative ions (ANIONS).
⚙️METALLOIDS (semi-metals) — Along the staircase. Have BOTH metal and nonmetal properties.
Metalloids: B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te (and sometimes At).
Exceptions at the staircase: Aluminum (Al) and Polonium (Po) touch the staircase but are classified as METALS.
Metalloids are excellent semiconductors — critical for computer chips!
❓ Quick Test:
5) Shiny, conducts electricity, does NOT shatter = METAL
6) Dull, does NOT conduct, shatters = NONMETAL
7) Shiny, DOES conduct, DOES shatter = METALLOID
💡 The ONE property NOT found in metals: BRITTLE. That's answer D on your Regents! (Metals are malleable — the opposite of brittle.)

Most-tested category facts: (1) Transition metals form colored compounds in solution — this appears on nearly every exam. (2) Bromine is the only nonmetal that is a liquid at STP. (3) Mercury (Hg) is the only metal that is a liquid at STP. (4) Al and Po touch the staircase but are classified as metals, not metalloids.

🔬 Why Are Metals Good Conductors?

Metals have a "sea of mobile valence electrons" — these outermost electrons aren't tightly bound to any one atom. They can move freely through the metallic lattice, carrying electrical charge (electricity) and thermal energy (heat). Nonmetals have tightly held electrons that can't move freely, making them poor conductors.

💻 Silicon Chips: Silicon (metalloid) is the backbone of modern electronics — its semiconductor properties let engineers control whether it conducts or not, perfect for transistors.
🌡️ Mercury Thermometers: Mercury (Hg) is a metal that is liquid at room temperature — its unique property makes it useful in thermometers and barometers.

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Named Groups (Families)

Topic 4
🏷️
Element Families
Groups with shared properties and special names
💥Group 1 — ALKALI METALS: Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr
1 valence electron. Extremely reactive — never found alone in nature. Easily LOSE their 1 electron to form +1 ions (cations). Most reactive metal = Cesium (Cs) — Francium is more reactive but too rare. React violently with water!
🌍Group 2 — ALKALINE EARTH METALS: Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra
2 valence electrons. Very highly reactive. Lose 2 electrons → +2 ions. Never found alone in nature. Examples: calcium in bones, magnesium in chlorophyll.
⚙️Groups 3–12 — TRANSITION METALS (middle of table)
Found in the MIDDLE of the table. Form COLORED compounds in solution (Cu = bright blue, Fe = orange-red). Tend to be hard and dense. LEAST reactive group of metals. Can form multiple positive ions (variable oxidation states).
🎯 REGENTS ALERT: Colored compounds in solution ALWAYS = transition metals!
🧪Group 17 — HALOGENS: F, Cl, Br, I, At
7 valence electrons. Gain 1 electron → -1 ions (anions). Most reactive nonmetal = FLUORINE. Form compounds called SALTS. Three states of matter at STP: F₂ & Cl₂ = gases, Br₂ = liquid, I₂ = solid.
👑Group 18 — NOBLE GASES (Inert Gases): He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn
Full valence shells (8 electrons — except He which has 2). Most STABLE group. Exist alone as single atoms (monoatomic). Do NOT react with other elements under normal conditions. Exception to Octet Rule: He has only 2 valence e⁻.
⚗️Lanthanides (57–71) & Actinides (89–103) — Inner Transition Metals
The two rows at the BOTTOM of the table. Also called inner transition metals. Actinides are mostly radioactive. They belong in periods 6 and 7 but are pulled out to save space.
🔑 Hydrogen Exception: H is in Group 1 but is NOT an alkali metal. It's a NONMETAL gas. It's placed in Group 1 because it also has 1 valence electron, but its properties are completely different from metals.

Memorize the five named groups and their valence counts: Alkali (1), Alkaline Earth (2), Transition Metals (variable — colored compounds!), Halogens (7), Noble Gases (8, He=2). Common Regents traps: Hydrogen is NOT an alkali metal. The most reactive metal is Cs (or Fr). The most reactive nonmetal is F. "Gains 1 e⁻ to form a −1 ion" → Halogen. "Forms colored compounds in solution" → Transition metal, every time.

🔬 Why Are Alkali Metals So Reactive?

Alkali metals have only 1 valence electron that is far from the nucleus (especially Cs which is Period 6). Low ionization energy means very little energy is needed to remove that electron. Once removed, the resulting ion has a full noble-gas configuration — very stable. This makes alkali metals eager to react and give away their electron to achieve stability.

🏮 Neon Signs: Noble gases (Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe) glow different colors when electricity passes through them — used in signs, lasers, and lighting. Their stability makes them safe for this use.
🦴 Calcium in Bones: Calcium (Group 2) forms Ca²⁺ ions that combine with phosphate to make hydroxyapatite — the mineral that gives bones and teeth their strength.
🔋 Lithium Batteries: Lithium (alkali metal) readily loses its 1 electron, making it excellent for rechargeable batteries in phones and electric cars.

The transition metals form colored ions in solution because of partially filled d-orbitals. Using Table S, identify which transition metal compound would produce a bright blue solution. Explain why the alkali metals do NOT form colored solutions.

Fluorine is the most reactive nonmetal and Cesium is the most reactive metal. Using what you know about electronegativity and ionization energy, explain why these two elements are at opposite "extremes" of reactivity.

⚡ Valence Electrons & Lewis Dot Diagrams

Topic 5
⚛️
Valence Electrons & Lewis Dots
The key to chemical reactivity
Valence electrons = the outermost electrons in an atom. They are the HIGHEST energy electrons and are the ones involved in chemical bonding.
How to find them: For main group elements, the group number = number of valence electrons (Groups 1–2 and 13–18).
Maximum valence electrons = 8 (the "octet"). The OCTET RULE: atoms want 8 valence electrons to achieve a noble gas configuration.
Exception: Period 1 elements (H and He) — a full shell = 2 electrons (not 8).
🎱"EIGHT IS GREAT!" — All atoms want 8 valence electrons (except Period 1)
Lewis Dot Diagrams:
1. Write the chemical symbol for the element
2. Place dots around the symbol — one dot per valence electron
3. Place one dot on each side before pairing up (4 sides: top, right, bottom, left)
Note: We ONLY show valence electrons in Lewis dot diagrams!
Lewis Dot Examples:
Na (Group 1) → Na• (1 dot)
Mg (Group 2) → •Mg• (2 dots)
C (Group 14) → 4 dots around C (one on each side)
O (Group 16) → 6 dots (2 paired + 2 paired + 2 singles — OR however arranged)
Cl (Group 17) → 7 dots
Ne (Group 18) → 8 dots (all paired)
💡 Elements in the SAME GROUP have the SAME Lewis dot diagram pattern — same number of dots, same arrangement! This is why they have similar chemical properties.

Lewis dot diagrams are drawn almost every year. Rules: (1) Group # = number of dots for main-group elements. (2) Place one dot per side first (top, right, bottom, left), then pair up. (3) He = 2 dots (full Period 1 shell). The Regents may give you a Lewis dot diagram and ask which element or group it belongs to — count the dots and match to the group number.

📈 Periodic Trends

Topic 6
📉
Atomic Radius
½ the distance between neighboring nuclei — size of the atom

🎨 Interactive Trend Visualizer

Select a trend to see how it changes across the periodic table:

⬇️Going DOWN a group → Atomic radius INCREASES
Why? Each period adds a new energy level (shell) to the outside of the atom. More shells = more space taken up = larger atom.
➡️Going ACROSS a period (L→R) → Atomic radius DECREASES
Why? Same number of energy levels across a period, but MORE protons are added. The nuclear charge (positive pull) increases, pulling all electrons in closer → smaller atom. Think of it like a stronger magnet pulling the electron cloud inward.
💡 Largest atom: Bottom-left of table (Cs or Fr). Smallest atom: Top-right (excluding noble gases) — Fluorine (F) or Helium (He).

Trends appear on 3–5 Regents questions every year, including extended response. Know both the direction AND the reason: Atomic radius decreases across a period because more protons pull electrons closer with the same number of shells. Electronegativity increases across a period for the same reason — fluorine (4.0) is the maximum. Ionization energy increases across a period because smaller atoms hold electrons tighter.

🔬 Shielding Effect

Inner-shell electrons "shield" the outer electrons from the full pull of the nucleus. As you go down a group, there are more inner electrons providing this shielding effect, which reduces the effective nuclear charge felt by valence electrons. This is another reason why atomic radius increases going down — valence electrons aren't pulled in as tightly.

Without looking at the table, predict which atom is larger: Sulfur (S, Period 3, Group 16) or Selenium (Se, Period 4, Group 16)? Explain using energy levels.

Compare the atomic radius of Na (Period 3, Group 1) and Cl (Period 3, Group 17). Both are in the same period — explain the difference using nuclear charge.

🔄 Ionic Radius & Isoelectronic Series

Topic 7
🔋
Cations, Anions & Isoelectronic Series
How ion formation changes size
➕CATION (loses e⁻) = always SMALLER than the neutral atom
Why? When an atom loses electrons, it has FEWER electrons being pulled by the SAME nuclear charge. The protons outnumber the electrons. The nucleus can pull the remaining electrons in closer → smaller ion.
Example: Na = 190 pm → Na⁺ = 116 pm
➖ANION (gains e⁻) = always LARGER than the neutral atom
Why? When an atom gains electrons, the electrons now OUTNUMBER the protons. The same nuclear charge is spread over more electrons — weaker pull per electron → electrons drift farther out → larger ion.
Example: Cl = 79 pm → Cl⁻ = 167 pm
🔄ISOELECTRONIC SERIES — same number of electrons, different identity
Atoms/ions that have the SAME number of electrons (same electron configuration) but DIFFERENT numbers of protons. They all have the configuration of the nearest noble gas.
Example: N³⁻, O²⁻, F⁻, Ne, Na⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺ — all have 10 electrons (Ne configuration).
In an isoelectronic series: More protons = smaller size (stronger pull on same electrons).
So: N³⁻ is LARGEST and Al³⁺ is SMALLEST in the Ne series.
🎯 Regents Tip: Group 17 elements (halogens) form ions that are LARGER than their neutral atoms (they gain electrons → anions). Group 1 elements form ions that are SMALLER than their neutral atoms (they lose electrons → cations).

Cation/anion size is tested nearly every year. Cations (lost e⁻) = SMALLER. Anions (gained e⁻) = LARGER. For isoelectronic series (same # of electrons), more protons = smaller radius. Example: Na⁺ is smaller than F⁻ — both have 10 electrons, but Na has 11 protons vs. F's 9. The Regents often gives a Reference Table S excerpt and asks you to rank ionic radii — always use proton count as the tiebreaker for isoelectronic sets.

🔬 Comparing Ions in an Isoelectronic Series

In an isoelectronic series, ALL ions have the same number of electrons. The key variable is the number of protons. More protons = stronger nuclear attraction pulling the same number of electrons inward = smaller ion. This is why Na⁺ (11 protons, 10 electrons) is smaller than F⁻ (9 protons, 10 electrons) even though both have 10 electrons.

Rank these ions from SMALLEST to LARGEST radius: Na⁺, Mg²⁺, F⁻, O²⁻, N³⁻. Explain your reasoning using the concept of nuclear charge vs. electron count.

A calcium atom has a radius of 194 pm and a calcium ion (Ca²⁺) has a radius of 100 pm. That's nearly half the size! Using your knowledge of nuclear charge and electrons, explain this dramatic size reduction.

🧲
Electronegativity & Ionization Energy
Attraction to electrons and energy to remove them
🧲ELECTRONEGATIVITY — attraction of an atom for electrons in a bond (Table S, range 0.7–4.0)
Most electronegative: Fluorine (F) = 4.0 (top-right)
Least electronegative: Francium (Fr) ≈ 0.7 (bottom-left)
Going across a period (L→R): INCREASES — nuclear charge increases, atoms are smaller, stronger pull on bonding electrons
Going down a group: DECREASES — valence electrons farther from nucleus, weaker attraction
⚡IONIZATION ENERGY — energy needed to REMOVE the most loosely bound electron (Table S, kJ/mol)
Going across a period (L→R): INCREASES — atoms are smaller, electrons held more tightly → more energy to remove
Going down a group: DECREASES — valence electrons are farther from nucleus and more shielded → easier to remove
Lowest ionization energy: Cs or Fr (bottom-left) — easiest to ionize = most reactive metals
Highest ionization energy: He and Ne (noble gases) — hardest to ionize = don't react
⚗️REACTIVITY — same trend as ionization energy (for metals) or electronegativity (for nonmetals)
Metals (lose electrons): Reactivity INCREASES going down a group (lower IE) and DECREASES going left→right across a period
Nonmetals (gain electrons): Reactivity INCREASES going up a group and going left→right across a period (toward Group 17)
⚠️ CANNOT compare metal reactivity to nonmetal reactivity directly!
💡 Metallic character (tendency to act like a metal) INCREASES going down a group and going left across a period — opposite of electronegativity!

Electronegativity and ionization energy questions often appear as extended response — you must explain the reason, not just the direction. Template answer: "As you go [direction], atomic radius [increases/decreases] because [more energy levels / more protons with same shells], so the valence electrons are [farther from / closer to] the nucleus, making it [easier/harder] to [remove/attract] them." Also remember: You CANNOT compare metal reactivity to nonmetal reactivity — metals react by losing e⁻, nonmetals by gaining them.

🔬 Period 3 Electronegativity Trend

From Na → Cl in Period 3: EN increases from 0.9 to 3.0. All atoms in Period 3 have electrons in the same 3 energy levels. But the nuclear charge goes from 11 (Na) to 17 (Cl). The increasing nuclear charge pulls bonding electrons toward the nucleus more strongly as you go right, resulting in higher electronegativity.

🔥 Why Cesium Explodes in Water: Cesium has the lowest ionization energy of any practical metal — its single valence electron is barely held. When it touches water, it gives up that electron almost instantly in a violent reaction, releasing hydrogen gas that ignites.
🦷 Fluoride in Toothpaste: Fluorine's extreme electronegativity makes it form incredibly strong bonds. When fluoride replaces hydroxide in tooth enamel (hydroxyapatite → fluorapatite), the resulting compound is much harder and more acid-resistant.

Element A has a first ionization energy of 496 kJ/mol and Element B has a first ionization energy of 1314 kJ/mol. Both are in the same period. Which is farther to the right in the period? Explain using atomic radius and nuclear charge.

Answer (1): As elements in Period 3 are considered in order of increasing atomic number, atomic radius DECREASES and electronegativity INCREASES. Explain the reasoning behind BOTH trends using a single underlying cause.

Classification
An element is a solid at STP, has metallic luster, and can be drawn into wire. It dissolves in water to form a brightly colored solution. What type of element is it, and what group does it most likely belong to?
1.Solid + metallic luster + can be drawn into wire (ductile) = METAL
2.Forms a COLORED solution when dissolved in water = TRANSITION METAL (Groups 3–12)
3.Answer: It is a TRANSITION METAL. Examples include Cu (blue solution), Fe (orange/green), Ni (green), Co (pink).
Periodic Trend
Rank the following elements in order of INCREASING atomic radius: F, Cl, Br, O
1.Identify positions: F (Period 2, Gr 17), O (Period 2, Gr 16), Cl (Period 3, Gr 17), Br (Period 4, Gr 17)
2.F and O are both in Period 2. F is to the right (Gr 17 vs Gr 16), so F is SMALLER than O across the period.
3.Going DOWN Group 17: F → Cl → Br, radius increases with each period.
4.Final order (smallest → largest): F < O < Cl < Br
Electron Config
An element has an electron configuration of 2-8-18-7. (a) What period is it in? (b) What group? (c) What is its name and symbol? (d) Draw its Lewis dot diagram.
a.Count the shells: 2-8-18-7 = 4 energy levels = Period 4
b.Last number (valence electrons) = 7 = Group 17 (Halogens)
c.Period 4, Group 17 = Bromine (Br)
d.Lewis dot: 7 valence electrons = 3 pairs + 1 single dot. Symbol Br with ·Br̈̈: (dots on all 4 sides, 3 sides with 2 each and 1 side with 1).
Ionic Radius
A magnesium atom (Mg) has an atomic radius of 145 pm. A Mg²⁺ ion has a radius of 86 pm. Explain why the ion is so much smaller, using the concepts of protons and electrons.
1.Neutral Mg: 12 protons, 12 electrons. The nuclear charge pulls on 12 electrons.
2.Mg²⁺: 12 protons, 10 electrons (lost 2). Same nuclear charge NOW pulling on only 10 electrons.
3.With 2 fewer electrons but the same 12 protons pulling inward, each remaining electron is pulled in much more tightly.
4.Result: Protons > Electrons → Cation → SMALLER radius (86 pm vs 145 pm)
Identification
An element at STP is a brittle solid that does NOT conduct electricity and has a dull appearance. It reacts by gaining 2 electrons. What is the most likely element? (Use the periodic table to narrow it down.)
1.Brittle + dull + poor conductor = NONMETAL
2.Solid at STP nonmetals include: C, S, P, I, and some others. (Most nonmetals are gases.)
3.Gains 2 electrons → forms -2 ion → Group 16 (chalcogens)
4.Solid + Group 16 = most likely Sulfur (S) — solid nonmetal in Group 16, Period 3.
Trend Direction
For Period 3 elements (Na through Ar), predict which property increases AND which property decreases as atomic number increases. Give the underlying reason for both trends.
Increases:Electronegativity & Ionization Energy — Both increase left→right across Period 3. Underlying cause: same number of energy levels, but nuclear charge (# protons) increases from 11 (Na) to 18 (Ar). More protons = stronger pull on electrons = harder to remove them and more attractive to bonding electrons.
Decreases:Atomic Radius & Metallic Character — Both decrease left→right. Nuclear charge increases pulls electrons closer = smaller atoms. Harder to lose electrons as you go right = less metallic behavior.
Root cause:ONE cause drives all these trends: Increasing nuclear charge (# protons) across the period with the same number of energy levels.
Lewis Dot
Draw the Lewis dot diagrams for: (a) K, (b) Ca, (c) Al, (d) P, (e) S, (f) Br. Then identify which pairs have the same number of dots.
a.K = Group 1 = 1 dot (K•)
b.Ca = Group 2 = 2 dots (•Ca•)
c.Al = Group 13 = 3 dots
d.P = Group 15 = 5 dots
e.S = Group 16 = 6 dots
f.Br = Group 17 = 7 dots
Same dots:K has same as Na, Li (all Group 1). P has same as N, As (all Group 15). Br has same as F, Cl, I (all Group 17). Same group = same Lewis dot pattern!
Group Identification
An aqueous (water) solution of a compound ZCl₂ appears bright blue. What group does element Z belong to? Give its name and a real-world use.
1.Bright blue solution = colored compound in solution = TRANSITION METAL (Groups 3–12)
2.The formula ZCl₂ means Z forms a +2 ion (since Cl is -1, and 2 Cl atoms = -2 total, Z must be +2).
3.A transition metal with a +2 oxidation state that forms a blue solution = Copper (Cu) — CuCl₂ is bright blue!
4.Real-world use: Copper is used in electrical wiring (excellent conductor), plumbing, and copper sulfate (blue) is used as a fungicide.
Isoelectronic
The following species are isoelectronic: S²⁻, Cl⁻, Ar, K⁺, Ca²⁺. (a) How many electrons does each have? (b) Rank them from largest to smallest radius.
a.S²⁻: 16+2=18 e⁻ | Cl⁻: 17+1=18 e⁻ | Ar: 18 e⁻ | K⁺: 19-1=18 e⁻ | Ca²⁺: 20-2=18 e⁻. All have 18 electrons!
b.Now look at protons (nuclear charge): S=16, Cl=17, Ar=18, K=19, Ca=20.
Rule:More protons = stronger pull on same # of electrons = SMALLER radius.
Ranking:S²⁻ > Cl⁻ > Ar > K⁺ > Ca²⁺ (largest to smallest)
History Application
Mendeleev left a gap in his table between calcium (Ca, atomic mass 40) and titanium (Ti, atomic mass 48). He predicted an element with atomic mass ~45, density ~3.5 g/cm³. In 1879, scandium (Sc) was discovered with atomic mass 45.0 and density 3.0 g/cm³. What does this tell us about Mendeleev's table?
1.Mendeleev predicted: mass ≈ 45, density ≈ 3.5. Actual scandium: mass = 45.0, density = 3.0.
2.His mass prediction was almost perfect. His density was close (3.5 vs 3.0 — only 17% off).
3.This demonstrates that the periodic table has PREDICTIVE POWER — the pattern of properties allows prediction of unknown elements.
Conclusion:Strong evidence that Mendeleev's arrangement was correct — periodic patterns are real, not coincidental.
Table S Practice
Using Table S (values below), answer: (a) Which has higher electronegativity — Na or Cl? (b) Which has higher ionization energy — K or Br? (c) Why is fluorine listed as the "most electronegative" element?
Na EN=0.9, Cl EN=3.0; K IE=419 kJ/mol, Br IE=1143 kJ/mol; F EN=4.0
a.Cl EN (3.0) > Na EN (0.9). Cl is more electronegative. Cl is on the right side of Period 3, Na is on the left. More nuclear charge = stronger pull on bonding electrons.
b.Br IE (1143) > K IE (419). Br has higher ionization energy. K is in Group 1 (easily loses its electron); Br is in Group 17 (strongly holds onto its electrons).
c.Fluorine's EN = 4.0, the highest on the scale. F is the smallest nonmetal with the highest nuclear charge relative to its size — it pulls bonding electrons toward itself more strongly than any other element.
Multi-Part Analysis
Compare potassium (K, Group 1, Period 4) and bromine (Br, Group 17, Period 4). They are both in Period 4. (a) Which has a larger atomic radius? (b) Which has higher electronegativity? (c) Which is more metallic? (d) Which is more reactive?
a.Atomic radius: K (Group 1) is on the LEFT. Br (Group 17) is on the RIGHT. Radius decreases left→right. K has the larger atomic radius.
b.Electronegativity increases left→right. Br has higher electronegativity (2.8 vs K's 0.8).
c.Metallic character decreases left→right. K is more metallic — it loses electrons easily; Br gains electrons (nonmetal).
d.Reactivity: K is a very reactive metal (easily loses 1 e⁻). Br is a reactive nonmetal. Cannot directly compare — different types! K is the most reactive METAL in Period 4; Br is the most reactive NONMETAL in Period 4 (after F and Cl).
Capstone
Element Q has these properties: Group 2, Period 5. (a) Write its electron configuration. (b) What is its name and symbol? (c) How many valence electrons? (d) What ion does it form? (e) Is it more or less reactive than calcium?
a.Period 5, Group 2 = 5 energy levels, 2 valence electrons. Configuration: 2-8-18-8-2
b.Period 5, Group 2 on the periodic table = Strontium (Sr)
c.Group 2 = 2 valence electrons
d.Alkaline earth metals lose 2 electrons = Sr²⁺ ion
e.Sr is in Period 5; Ca is in Period 4 (same Group 2). Going DOWN Group 2, reactivity increases. Sr is MORE reactive than Ca.
Regents Extended
The element germanium (Ge) was predicted by Mendeleev as "eka-silicon" before it was discovered. He predicted: atomic mass ~72, density ~5.5 g/cm³, oxide formula GeO₂. Based on its position (Group 14, Period 4), would you classify Ge as a metal, nonmetal, or metalloid? Explain TWO pieces of evidence for your classification.
1.Ge location: Group 14, Period 4 — this places it right on or near the STAIRCASE of the periodic table.
2.Classification: METALLOID (semi-metal)
Evidence 1:Ge is located along the staircase (between metals on the left and nonmetals on the right) — the defining position of metalloids.
Evidence 2:Ge has properties of BOTH metals (can conduct electricity under some conditions — semiconductor) AND nonmetals (brittle, not malleable). This dual nature is the hallmark of metalloids.
Bonus:Mendeleev correctly predicted its oxide formula GeO₂ — another triumph for the periodic table's predictive power!
Trend Application
Using the periodic trends, without looking up values, predict which element has a HIGHER first ionization energy: (a) Li or Na? (b) Na or Cl? (c) Ar or Cl? Justify each answer.
a.Li (Period 2) vs Na (Period 3) — same Group 1. Going DOWN, IE decreases. Li has HIGHER IE. Na's valence electron is farther from the nucleus (more energy levels), easier to remove.
b.Na (Group 1) vs Cl (Group 17) — same Period 3. Going RIGHT, IE increases. Cl has HIGHER IE. Cl is near the noble gas configuration — electrons held tightly. Na barely holds its 1 valence electron.
c.Ar (Group 18) vs Cl (Group 17) — same Period 3. Noble gases have the HIGHEST IE in each period. Ar has HIGHER IE. Full valence shell = maximum electron stability = hardest to remove an electron.
Concept Check
An element forms an ion with a charge of 3+. Its electron configuration as an ION is 2-8. (a) How many electrons does the neutral atom have? (b) What is its atomic number? (c) What element is it? (d) What group and period?
a.Ion has 2-8 = 10 electrons. It lost 3 (3+ charge). Neutral atom had 10 + 3 = 13 electrons
b.Atomic number = # protons = # electrons in neutral atom = 13
c.Atomic number 13 = Aluminum (Al)
d.Al configuration: 2-8-3 → 3 energy levels = Period 3; 3 valence electrons = Group 13, Period 3
Ranking Challenge
Rank these elements from MOST to LEAST reactive as METALS: Li, Cs, K, Na, Rb. Explain your reasoning.
1.All are in Group 1 (Alkali Metals). For metals, reactivity increases GOING DOWN a group.
2.Order by period (top to bottom): Li (Period 2), Na (Period 3), K (Period 4), Rb (Period 5), Cs (Period 6).
3.Lower period = easier to lose valence electron (farther from nucleus, lower IE) = more reactive.
Ranking:Cs > Rb > K > Na > Li (most to least reactive)
Periodic Law
The Periodic Law states that properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic number. Using Li (Z=3), Na (Z=11), and K (Z=19): each has 1 valence electron and forms +1 ions. Explain how this supports the Periodic Law.
1.Li (Z=3), Na (Z=11), K (Z=19) — atomic numbers increase by ~8 each time (they're in periods 2, 3, 4).
2.Despite very different atomic numbers (3, 11, 19), all three have the SAME number of valence electrons (1) and form the SAME type of ion (+1).
3.The property (1 valence electron) REPEATS at regular intervals as atomic number increases.
Conclusion:This demonstrates that chemical properties repeat periodically — every time a new period begins (filled energy level), the count of valence electrons "resets" to 1.
Hydrogen Exception
Hydrogen is placed in Group 1 of the periodic table but is NOT an alkali metal. Give THREE pieces of evidence that show hydrogen is different from the alkali metals (Li, Na, K, etc.).
1.Physical state: H₂ is a GAS at STP. All alkali metals are SOLIDS at STP. This is a fundamental physical difference.
2.Metal vs. Nonmetal: Hydrogen is classified as a NONMETAL. Alkali metals are, well, metals — they have luster, are malleable, and conduct electricity. H has none of these properties.
3.Reactivity pattern: Alkali metals react violently with water (releasing H₂ gas). Hydrogen itself IS the gas released — it's a product of the reaction, not a reactant like alkali metals are. H also behaves as a nonmetal in bonding (forms covalent bonds).
Why in Group 1?Same reason for placement: 1 valence electron. But placement does NOT mean identical properties.
Capstone Regents
[Regents-style] The table below shows first ionization energies for Period 3 elements: Na=496, Mg=738, Al=578, Si=786, P=1012, S=1000, Cl=1251, Ar=1521 (kJ/mol). (a) What is the general trend? (b) Why does Al (578) have a LOWER IE than Mg (738) even though Al is farther right? (c) Why does S (1000) have a LOWER IE than P (1012)?
a.General trend: IE generally INCREASES across Period 3 from Na to Ar. More protons = stronger hold on electrons.
b.Mg (Group 2): [Ne]3s². Al (Group 13): [Ne]3s²3p¹. The p-orbital electron in Al is slightly HIGHER in energy than the s-orbital electrons in Mg. It's easier to remove a p-orbital electron than an s-orbital electron. Al's first electron to be removed is in a higher-energy, less-stable p orbital → lower IE than Mg.
c.P (Group 15): [Ne]3s²3p³ — each p-orbital has ONE electron (half-filled = extra stable). S (Group 16): [Ne]3s²3p⁴ — one p-orbital has TWO electrons that repel each other, making ONE easier to remove. S has paired electrons in one p-orbital → repulsion makes one electron easier to remove → slightly lower IE than P.

👩‍🏫 Teacher Notes

Unit 3

⚠️ Common Misconceptions

❌ "Atomic radius increases as you go across a period." — Students confuse period and group trends. Across a period, MORE protons pull electrons IN → radius DECREASES. Down a group, MORE energy levels push electrons OUT → radius increases.
❌ "Sodium and bromine are comparable in reactivity." — You CANNOT compare a metal's reactivity to a nonmetal's reactivity directly. Metals react by losing electrons; nonmetals by gaining them. This is a classic Regents trap.
❌ "Noble gases have the largest atomic radius in their period." — Noble gases are actually SMALLER than expected because they have the most protons in their period pulling in the electrons. The trend of decreasing radius across a period continues through the noble gases.
❌ "Hydrogen is an alkali metal." — Hydrogen is in Group 1 only because it has 1 valence electron. It is a NONMETAL. Reinforce the staircase rule and the exception list.
❌ "Cations are larger than neutral atoms." — It's the opposite. Cations LOSE electrons → same nuclear pull on fewer electrons → smaller. Anions GAIN electrons → same pull on more electrons → larger.
❌ "All elements along the staircase are metalloids." — Aluminum (Al) and Polonium (Po) touch the staircase but are classified as METALS. Only the diagonal elements B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te (and sometimes At) are metalloids.

Malleable, ductile, luster, conductors
Lose e⁻ → CATIONS (+)
Solids at STP (except Hg = liquid)
Exception: H is in Group 1 but is a NONMETAL

💨 Nonmetals (right of staircase)

Brittle, dull, poor conductors
Gain e⁻ → ANIONS (-)
Mostly gases at STP (Br₂ = liquid; I₂, S, C = solids)

⚙️ Metalloids (on staircase)

B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te
BOTH metal and nonmetal properties
Semiconductors
Exceptions: Al and Po = METALS

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Named Groups

Gr 1: Alkali Metals (1 valence e⁻, most reactive metals)
Gr 2: Alkaline Earth Metals (2 valence e⁻)
Gr 3–12: Transition Metals (colored compounds!)
Gr 17: Halogens (7 valence e⁻, F most reactive nonmetal)
Gr 18: Noble Gases (full shells, unreactive)

📈 Periodic Trends

Atomic radius: ↓ group ↑ | → period ↓
Electronegativity: ↓ group ↓ | → period ↑ (F=4.0 max)
Ionization Energy: ↓ group ↓ | → period ↑
Metallic character: ↓ group ↑ | → period ↓
Metal reactivity: ↓ group ↑ | Nonmetal reactivity: ↓ group ↓

🔋 Ionic Radius

Cation (lost e⁻): SMALLER than neutral atom
→ same protons, fewer electrons → pulled in closer
Anion (gained e⁻): LARGER than neutral atom
→ same protons, more electrons → spread out farther
Isoelectronic: same e⁻ count, more protons = smaller

⚡ Valence Electrons

= outermost electrons = highest energy electrons
Group number (main group) = # valence e⁻
Period number = # occupied energy levels
OCTET RULE: want 8 valence e⁻ (except Period 1 = 2)
Lewis dots: one dot per valence e⁻ around symbol
